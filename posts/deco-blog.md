test  At first glance, phrases like “user-centred approach” and “bespoke features” can sound like standard product language. But in the context of BlaBla, they signal something more demanding: not just building another platform, but redefining where value actually sits in the used car journey.

There is no shortage of places to buy and sell cars. Platforms like Facebook Marketplace, Gumtree, and CarsGuide already handle transactions efficiently. They solve for visibility and access.

What they don’t solve is confidence.

Buying a used car is not a simple transaction — it’s a high-stakes, emotionally charged decision shaped by uncertainty and information asymmetry. Buyers worry about hidden issues, misleading listings, and whether they’re making the “right” choice. Sellers, on the other hand, often lack clarity on how to price their vehicle in a way that reflects real market demand rather than guesswork.

This is where the distinction in the brief becomes critical: BlaBla is positioned as a community platform, not a marketplace.

That shifts the focus entirely.

Instead of facilitating transactions, the platform should support decision-making — specifically, by surfacing the kind of knowledge that traditional marketplaces fail to capture.

The key gaps are clear:

For buyers: access to real-world ownership insights — how a car actually performs over time, common faults, maintenance realities, and whether a seller seems credible
For sellers: access to grounded pricing intelligence — what similar cars are realistically selling for right now, and how to position their listing accordingly

If the community can generate and organise this kind of insight, it creates a layer of value that transactional platforms simply don’t offer. It becomes a space where users reduce risk, build trust, and make more informed decisions.

Accessibility (AA compliance), initially framed as a constraint, reinforces this direction rather than limiting it. The used car audience is inherently broad — spanning different age groups, levels of digital literacy, language backgrounds, and physical abilities. If the platform is not inclusive by design, it undermines its own purpose as a community resource.

In that sense, accessibility is not an add-on — it is foundational to trust, reach, and usability.
